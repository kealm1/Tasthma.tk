Thunderstorm Asthma Overview

Our climate in Victoria varies – it perpetually has and is expected to continue to do so. This temperamental nature of our climate results in periods that are cooler and wetter than average, while others are hotter and drier. Notwithstanding, the long-term average is changing in reaction to climate change. Experts expect that our future climate will be dramatically different from past experiences. Moreover the incidence and magnitude of extreme weather events are forecasted to rise.

In the last two decades Victoria has encountered numerous extreme weather events and climatic trends. An extraordinary series of extreme weather events were thunderstorm asthma outbreaks. Now classified as an epidemic, thunderstorm asthma, is the occurrence of an immense number of community members developing indications of asthma in a short interval. Current investigations propose that epidemic thunderstorm asthma is predominantly stimulated by an unusual mingling of high pollen levels and a distinct category of thunderstorm.

In particular, the thunderstorm on Monday, 21 November 2016 generated an unexpected and devastating thunderstorm asthma epidemic in Melbourne and Geelong. It was the evening of the very first hot day when Victorians experienced severe thunderstorm activity, reasonably normal for the state’s climate. This storm advanced from the west of Victoria, through Geelong and across to Melbourne. The storm swept grass pollen grain into the clouds consequently triggering a catastrophic occurrence - an epidemic thunderstorm asthma event. An outbreak of this intensity and magnitude had never been expected or encountered and so not planned for. In a very short span of time thousands of Victorians experienced breathing difficulties. Most of these individuals had neither been diagnosed with asthma nor experienced an asthma attack.
